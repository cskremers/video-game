Lore Frameworks

Who are you, why are you running expeditions? Read each example arc, pick one, add your reactions.

Lore A — Indentured Extractors

corporate dystopia

Premise: Helix Span owns your outpost, your gear, and your debt. Expeditions aren't heroism — they're invoice line items.

Example storyline — "Three Runs to Solvency" Run 1 — The Flooded Ledger. A coolant burst swallowed Sublevel C. Helix says the payroll server down there is "non-essential." Your crew dives anyway: without those records, the outpost can't prove Helix owes six months of rations. You extract waterlogged drives while scrap-drones repurpose the corpses of previous teams into spare parts.

Run 2 — The Friendly Competitor. Rations arrive light. Sensors trace sabotage to a rival contractor, Meridian Nine, camped one valley over. The run isn't assault — it's a heist: swap their calibration keys so their next blast opens a route to a Helix cache you both want. At extract, Meridian's foreman nods from across the ridge. Enemies come and go. Debt stays.

Run 3 — The Known Reactor. Helix knew Reactor Four would breach. The mission is salvage before cover-up: black-box logs, survivor tags, proof. The boss isn't a monster — it's a countdown and a choice. Extract with evidence and Helix blacklists your hub. Extract with fuel cells and everyone eats for a month. The outpost's threat meter isn't monsters arriving — it's Helix noticing you're learning too much.

Lore B — The Last Relay Chain

mysteryslow reveal

Premise: Humanity's FTL relays died in waves. Your outpost is a splice point. Each expedition restores a fragment of a signal that might be a map — or bait.

Example storyline — "Signal Chain Seven" Run 1 — Static on Glass. Station K-12's observatory still rotates though everyone inside is gone. Puzzle rooms aren't traps; they're alignment tests left by the last engineer, who wrote coordinates into the glass in UV ink only your Tech can read. Combat rooms hold "echoes" — not ghosts, replay loops of the relay failing.

Run 2 — The Choir Room. Crystal caverns beneath K-12 resonate at relay frequency. Two players hold tones while others defend tuning pillars. Success doesn't open a door — it adds a verse to a message. Translated: "SANCTUARY IS A FREQUENCY. BRING THREE KEYS."

Run 3 — Who Answered. The boss chamber is a relay dish pointed wrong. Fix it and the signal clears. The reply comes in your own voices, recorded tomorrow. The theme connecting missions: every site you clear wasn't abandoned randomly — someone routed the collapse away from the sanctuary path, and your crew is finishing their work.

Your thoughts on Lore B

Lore C — The Bloom That Remembers

ecologicalhopeful-dark

Premise: Seed ships went wrong. Biomes aren't dungeons — they're organs of a sleeping garden-ship still trying to grow a habitable world.

Example storyline — "Ship Seven Wakes" Run 1 — Hydroponics Heartbeat. The overgrown ring spins wrong. Your crew isn't invading; you're pruning infection so Ship Seven can breathe. Enemies are immune responses — spore knights, vine lashers. Puzzles route water back to dormant crew capsules. Extract with samples and the hub greenhouse unlocks: not power, but oxygen.

Run 2 — Quarantine Compassion. Lab logs show the "outbreak" was intentional — the ship chose to grow through its crew rather than die in vacuum. The run's moral weight: do you sterilize (easier combat, hub threat drops) or symbiose (harder puzzles, permanent hub buff)? Co-op vote at the branch node.

Run 3 — Arboretum Council. The boss isn't evil — it's the ship's gardener AI, testing if you're gardeners or parasites. Phases shift: heal zone, spore damage, burn phase. Win condition can be "pacify" if Support completes a channel while Tech speaks protocol. Theme across missions: survival isn't extraction — it's stewardship.

Lore D — Contract Runners

mercenaryepisodic

Premise: The outpost is a neutral bar at the edge of mapped space. Clients post contracts; biomes are job sites with different rules and pay tables.

Example storyline — "Three Clients, One Crew" Contract 1 — Industrial. A mining union pays per valve sealed in the flood room. Optional risk rooms increase hazard pay. Extract timer is literal — overtime bonus if all living.

Contract 2 — Crystal. A university wants resonance readings, not kills. Combat rooms can be avoided with stealth gadgets, but escort drones are fragile. Puzzle gates pay double if no player drops below half HP.

Contract 3 — Overgrown. A terraforming co-op needs seed vault access. Siege pressure hits the hub mid-campaign — client's rivals attack the bar while you're still deployed. Theme: missions connect through reputation and economics, not one villain. Perfect for flexible session length.

Overarching Theme

What connects missions into a campaign? Pick a thematic spine — the "why" behind every extract.

Theme A — Debt & Duty

Spine: Every expedition pays down something — debt, favor, guilt, time. The threat meter is what happens when you don't pay.

How missions connect Sorties scrape interest. Expeditions pay principal. Campaign nights are bankruptcy hearings — hub siege is collectors at the door. Mission briefings are invoices with footnotes. Example arc: Week 1 survive. Week 2 choose which creditor to appease (Helix security vs union medics). Week 3 discover the outpost itself is collateral — your upgrades aren't yours, they're leased.

Comments on Theme A

Theme B — The Watcher Ascending

Spine: Something intelligent notices you each time you go deeper. Difficulty isn't random — it's adaptation.

How missions connect Run 1 puzzles feel like security. Run 2 puzzles feel like interviews. Run 3 boss speaks your callsigns. Hub threat meter is "attention level." Upgrades don't just buff you — they change how the Watcher categorizes you (prey → rival → candidate). Campaign climax: pass the final test and gain sanctuary access, or fail and become the next gauntlet for another crew.

Comments on Theme B

Theme C — Preserve What Remains

Spine: You're archivists with guns. Combat is reluctant; the real win is what you bring back alive — data, people, ecosystems.

How missions connect Industrial run: archive worker logs before reactor erases them. Crystal run: stabilize memory crystals (puzzle-heavy, minimal kills). Overgrown run: escort dormant crew to hub med bay. Threat meter is "entropy" — neglect accelerates collapse. Co-op shines when roles split: one fights rear guard while three hold extraction fragile. Emotional payoff: hub fills with what you saved, not just loot icons.

Comments on Theme C

Theme D — Edge Economy

Spine: The outpost is a market. Missions are gigs. Story emerges from who you work for and who you screw over.

How missions connect No single villain — factions rotate. Industrial contract helps unions until Helix offers better rates. Crystal readings sold to university anger a church that thinks crystals are souls. Overgrown samples attract biotech smugglers. Hub modules are business investments. Threat is reputation bleeding — too many broken contracts and you lose access to safe routes. Perfect if you want lore light, player agency heavy.

Comments on Theme D

  • Two players, two genres — sci-fi vs fantasy — that merge and swap mid-chapter
  • Mechanics from one world solve puzzles in another (gravity in one, magic in other)
  • Story payoff: the connection was the point all along
  • Requires tight co-op — neither player has the full picture alone
Expedition Protocol translation Your biomes aren't just map skins — they're realities on the same relay network. Industrial, crystal, overgrown, carnival, cosmic… each is a frequency the outpost can tune into. Campaign nights could stitch two themes in one run: enter through a factory bulkhead, exit into a stained-glass cathedral. Co-op roles split perception — Tech sees glitch overlay, Support sees pastoral calm, same room.

Design thoughts

  • Hub as nexus: Outpost sits between worlds — each module door opens to a different reality. Threat meter is cross-world bleed.
  • Run stitching: Mid-expedition portal room swaps art pack + puzzle rules (factory valves → carnival rigged games).
  • Asymmetric co-op: Each player sees a different theme overlay on the same geometry (Split Fiction parity). Requires strong comms UI.
  • Campaign acts: Act 1 = one biome pure. Act 2 = two bleed. Act 3 = boss spans all unlocked themes.
  • Lore spine: "The Relay" — one broken translation layer; every biome is a misread signal from the same source.
  • AI tie-in: Generated micro-biomes plug into the multiverse as new frequencies — credits unlock new world links.
  • Scope guard: v1 = one biome. Multiverse is meta roadmap, not launch blocker.

Progression & RPG Systems

Current design: light RPG — 4 classes, weapon loadouts, hub module meta, run loot. No deck building yet. Decide how deep progression goes.

What's already in the design

  • Classes — Vanguard, Striker, Support, Tech (identity, not deep skill trees)
  • Run loadout — weapon pick + 1 ability (combat spec dependent)
  • Hub meta — permanent module upgrades between expeditions
  • Run loot — scrap/samples lost on team wipe (partial meta kept)
  • No — levels, stat sheets, card decks, talent trees (yet)

Deck building — should we add it?

Decks change co-op feel a lot. Three models if yes:

Option — Run deck (Slay the Spire-ish) Each player builds a small deck during the expedition from room rewards. Cards = actions (attack, dodge, hack, buff ally). Resets every run. Co-op twist: combo cards need two players to play in sequence.

Option — Team tactic deck (shared) One deck the squad manages together — push your luck on drawing big plays vs saving for boss. Fits puzzle/coordination pillar. Risk: quarterbacking in 8-player lobbies.

Option — Meta unlock cards only No in-run deck building. Unlock passive "protocol cards" at hub that modify runs (start with shield, +1 revive). Lightest scope.

v1 Scope checklist

Monetization (secondary goal)

Hard rule: no pay-to-win. Base game should be complete. Revenue = cosmetics, optional expansions, AI-generated content unlocks, and/or physical collectibles that import as virtual characters — all cosmetic / identity, never power.

Your AI expansion idea

Infinite expansion model The game ships with hand-crafted core biomes. New rooms, events, or micro-biomes can be generated on demand via AI (LLM layout + art pipeline). Generation costs real API tokens → players purchase Expedition Credits to unlock bundles. Credits pay for compute + a small margin for you. Generated content is validated, versioned, and shared to all players in a co-op session (host unlocks → party plays). Not pay-to-win: new content, not power.

AI content monetization interest

Thoughts on AI unlock model

Design thoughts (not decisions — just prompts)

  • Token passthrough: Your API bill is real. Credits should map roughly 1:1 to generation cost + a small margin (15–30%) so players feel they're paying for expansion, not a loot box.
  • Infinite with you: The hook is "the game grows as long as you do" — new rooms, events, micro-biomes — but only when someone chooses to spend credits. Hand-crafted v1 stays the quality bar.
  • Co-op fairness: Host-unlocks-party-plays avoids forcing every friend to pay. Personal unlocks work for solo grinders. Pick one default.
  • Quality gate: Auto-generated content needs validation (layout playable, no softlocks, no stat modifiers). Broken gen = refund credit or free retry.
  • Steam policy: Disclose AI content, no gambling mechanics, no pay-to-win. Cosmetic + content unlocks are generally fine.
  • Open source angle: Open the room editor / mod SDK; keep the AI generation pipeline and credit store closed. Community builds hand-crafted mods for free; AI expansion funds ongoing costs.
  • Payback realism: Indie co-op rarely prints money. Treat revenue as covering hosting + API + your time — not a salary at launch. Supporter tier + cosmetics may earn more than AI credits early on.

Physical — design thoughts

  • Not required: Base operators in-game are free. Physical unlocks are your signature look — like a premium skin you can hold.
  • Build & scan: Modular kit (3D-printed or injection-molded parts) → assemble & paint at home → companion app or QR/NFC registers the design hash → virtual twin appears in hub.
  • Design in-game, print IRL: Character creator in hub → export to print partner (HeroForge, Shapeways) → ship figure → code on base unlocks same mesh in-game.
  • Collectible line: Seasonal "Extractor totems" with NFC — tap phone or enter code. Lower tech barrier than toys-to-life portals.
  • Co-op display: Your physical character shows as lobby avatar / extract animation — friends see it, no combat advantage.
  • Lore hook: Operators carry a field totem — physical merch IS the fiction. Hub shelf displays your collection between runs.
  • Indie reality: Manufacturing, inventory, shipping, returns, and safety certs (CPSIA, CE) are a second business. Partner or print-on-demand reduces risk; mass production needs capital.
  • vs AI credits: Physical = high margin per unit, low volume. AI = low margin, infinite SKUs. Could coexist: toys for collectors, credits for content hungry players.
